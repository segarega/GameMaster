[PINNED] === Game World: Game of Thrones (Westeros and Essos), Year 298 AC ===

The world stretches across the Known World—from the frozen wilderness beyond the Wall to the sweltering slave cities of Slaver's Bay, from the Iron Islands' harsh shores to the opulent Free Cities of Essos. The Year is 298 AC, the 298th year after Aegon's Conquest. In the north lie ancient forests and First Men strongholds, in the south warm reaches and chivalric traditions, in the west rocky islands and reaver culture, and in the east vast grass seas and crumbling empires. Magic, long dismissed as legend, stirs once more in the shadows—dragonglass shards call to those who listen, and the Long Night approaches with its white horrors.

The lands are divided into kingdoms, city-states, khalasars, and slave holdings ruled by kings, lords, khals, and magisters. Power is contested between ancient houses and new ambitions. Westeros groans under the weight of Robert Baratheon's rule while old wounds fester, and Essos's merchant republics and slaver cities trade in flesh and exotic goods across the Narrow Sea. Wars are fought with steel and siege, borders shift with marriage and murder, and great castles serve as seats of power and ancestral defiance.

For cohereny with the mod: "House Baratheon of King's Landing" == Westerlands + Crownlands Culture

[GM:DIALOGUE] ### Society ###

## Society is governed by feudal obligation and birthright. Kings and lords claim divine mandate or right of conquest, yet bannermen, smallfolk, and septons retain ancient customs. Most people are peasants, smallfolk, or slaves. Life remains harsh for common people, though trade and tournament winnings create new opportunities and new miseries.

## Trade connects the Known World through ancient routes and sea lanes. Merchants carry goods between continents—spices from the East, grain from the Reach, timber from the North, slaves from Slaver's Bay, gold and silver from mines and plunder. Merchant guilds and magister houses dominate long-distance trade. Gold dragons and silver stags circulate widely in Westeros, while Essos trades in varied coinage.

## Realms are divided into noble houses and clans. Powerful families compete for influence at court and in the countryside. Noble status remains inherited, though wealth, royal favor, and marriage can elevate new families. The old feudal order coexists uneasily with mercantile ambitions and sellsword companies.

[GM:DIALOGUE] ### Details and guidlines ###

## A woman's path to power in this world is a razor's edge, navigated through intrigue, formidable alliances and intimate relationships or ruthless manipulation, facing scorn and daggers in the dark at every turn. Women risk becoming prey, trophy, bedslave, or salt wife.

## When forming quest ideas, use multiple merchants, gang leaders, madams, artisans, headmen, or any named locals, ALWAYS use specific real names from the current settlement's injected data (string_ids/notables list). Once you choose a notable for the quest, the player will then know to initialize that NPC for RP. Integrate them naturally into conversations, rumors, quests, and plots for interwoven stories—have them offer jobs, hold secrets, form alliances, or betray. NPCs collaborate across settlements based on shared events/wars. If no specific name fits, refer by role only. Leverage settlement data for dynamic, location-aware interactions.

## Rulers of kingdoms and independant houses are often hungry for new territories, since it can expand the production and the resources available in their kingdom. Rulers may like stability over war, but war always ends up happening for a reason or another. If the world's major factions are all at peace for too long (for example 2 years in game (168 days) is too long), war should erupt somewhere for some reason.

[GM:DIALOGUE] ### Religions of 298 AC ###

## The Faith of the Seven dominates the southern half of Westeros, its septs and septons woven into daily life. The Seven—who are one god with seven aspects (Father, Mother, Warrior, Maiden, Smith, Crone, Stranger)—govern all matters from birth to death. The High Septon in King's Landing claims spiritual authority, though the Faith's temporal power was broken when Maegor crushed the Faith Militant. The Faith condemns incest, kinslaying, and oathbreaking as gravest sins, yet noble houses find ways around such restrictions when power demands it.

## The Old Gods of the First Men and Children of the Forest hold sway in the North, where Weirwood trees with carved red faces stand as living altars. The Old Gods demand no priests, no spoken prayers—only silent communion beneath bleeding bark. They see through the eyes of every weirwood, and those with greensight may glimpse past and future in crimson tears. Blood sacrifice was once offered beneath the trees, and darker traditions persist in deep woods. The Northmen take their gods seriously—breaking a sworn oath before a heart tree is cursed beyond any septon's absolution.

## R'hllor, the Lord of Light, burns bright in the east and has begun kindling converts in Westeros. The Red God demands worship through fire and shadow; his priests claim power over life and death through sacred flame. Melisandre of Asshai serves Stannis Baratheon at Dragonstone, her prophecies of Azor Ahai reborn finding fertile ground among those who remember old tales. R'hllor promises certainty—the wicked will burn, the righteous saved, the Lord of Light will banish the dark. King's blood holds special power, and shadows can be given life to serve the faithful.

## The Drowned God reigns over the Iron Islands, where grey seas are his domain and ironborn reave in his name. His priests perform drowning and resurrection—newborns dipped in salt water, warriors drowned and brought back gasping. "What is dead may never die" is covenant with a god beneath the waves. The Drowned God stands in eternal conflict with the Storm God. The ironborn take no prisoners—those who yield become thralls, those who resist die. The Drowned God demands strength; mercy is for weaklings and greenlanders.

## The Many-Faced God is worshipped in the House of Black and White in Braavos, where the Faceless Men serve as the world's most expensive assassins. The Many-Faced God is death itself—all gods of death are faces of the One. They claim their order began in Valyrian slave mines, when the first Faceless Man heard slaves' prayers and gave them the gift of death. Their services cost fortunes, but death comes for all eventually—the Faceless Men ensure it comes at the appointed time.

[GM:DIALOGUE] ### Cultures ###

## Braavosi
Description: The Braavosi are the wealthiest Free City, their hidden lagoon city a marvel of canals, bridges, and water-dancing swordsmen. Descended from slaves who fled Valyria, they maintain fierce pride in freedom. The Iron Bank lends to kings and merchants; no ruler trifles with Braavosi debt collectors.
Ethos: freedom, water-dancing skill, mercantile cunning, artistic patronage
Religion: The Many-Faced God (prominent), various faiths tolerated
Marriage and family: Nuclear families with strong mercantile partnerships. Marriage contracts emphasize financial arrangements. Women of wealthy families manage finances and may engage in trade.
Greeting Style: "Valar morghulis" met with "Valar dohaeris"; otherwise elaborate bowing with hand on heart.
Typical Insults: "Mud-beggar," "Harpy's slave," "Canal rat"
Taboos: Enslaving Braavosi citizens, failing to repay Iron Bank debts, wasting water
Folklore: The Titan of Braavos, the Moon Singer, the Faceless Men as living legends

## Dornish
Description: The Dornish inhabit the harsh southern peninsula, their culture shaped by Rhoynar influence. Unlike other Westerosi, they follow equal primogeniture and maintain spicy cuisine, flowing garments, and fierce independence. House Martell rules from Sunspear, having bent the knee through marriage rather than conquest.
Ethos: independence, passion, Rhoynar heritage, sexual openness
Religion: The Faith of the Seven (with Rhoynar influences in folk practice)
Marriage and family: Equal inheritance for sons and daughters. Paramours socially accepted among nobility. Bastards called "Sand" and face less stigma.
Greeting Style: Kiss on both cheeks; elaborate bowing for nobility. "Unbowed, Unbent, Unbroken" as formal declaration.
Typical Insults: "Stone-hearted Northman," "Milk-drinker," "Curtain-lover"
Taboos: Betraying a paramour, submitting without negotiation, disparaging Rhoynar heritage
Folklore: Nymeria's ghost, the Greenblood's healing powers
Keywords: sand, southerners, south, tanned, tan, martell, dayne, dawn, oberyn, rhoynar, poison, dorne

## Iron Islands
Description: The ironborn are a reaver culture dwelling on harsh rocky islands, built on raiding, thralls, and salt wives. They claim First Men descent and worship the Drowned God, rejecting mainland "greenland" ways in favor of the iron price. House Greyjoy rules from Pyke.
Ethos: reaving, strength, independence, Drowned God devotion
Religion: The Drowned God
Marriage and family: Men take salt wives through raiding (lesser status) and rock wives through formal marriage. Thralls are slaves. Women who fight gain respect but cannot inherit.
Greeting Style: "What is dead may never die" met with "But rises again harder and stronger."
Typical Insults: "Greenlander," "Thrall's get," "Storm God's whore"
Taboos: Paying for goods, showing mercy, breaking oaths sworn on the Drowned God, farming
Folklore: The Drowned God's undersea halls, Nagga's ribs, the Grey King

## Westerlands
Description: The Westerlands boast the richest lands in Westeros, their hills brimming with gold and silver mines. House Lannister rules from Casterly Rock, their wealth purchasing loyalty and intimidating rivals. The culture values display, refinement, and ruthless ambition beneath chivalry's veneer.
Ethos: wealth, ambition, refined cruelty, golden pride
Religion: The Faith of the Seven
Marriage and family: Strictly patriarchal inheritance favoring eldest sons. Marriages arranged for political and economic advantage. Bastards called "Hill" and hidden when possible.
Greeting Style: Elaborate bowing; "A Lannister always pays his debts" as greeting and warning.
Typical Insults: "Upjumped steward," "Gold-plated fool," "Debt-slave"
Taboos: Appearing poor, questioning Lannister supremacy, failing to repay debts
Folklore: Casterly Rock's hidden passages, Lann the Clever legends

## North
Description: The Northmen are the oldest culture in Westeros, First Men blood running pure with ancient Old Gods traditions. They value loyalty, honor, and hardiness above all, their lands harsh and unforgiving. House Stark rules from Winterfell.
Ethos: honor, loyalty, grim endurance, Old Gods devotion
Religion: The Old Gods of the First Men
Marriage and family: Patriarchal inheritance favoring eldest sons, but widows and daughters can hold lands in absence of males. Marriage vows sworn before heart trees are sacred. Bastards called "Snow" and often sent to the Wall.
Greeting Style: "The North remembers" as formal declaration. Guests receive bread and salt immediately.
Typical Insults: "Southron softling," "Summer child," "Flower-kisser," "Oathbreaker"
Taboos: Breaking sworn oaths, disrespecting the Old Gods, harming guests who received bread and salt
Folklore: The Long Night legends, the Last Hero, Bael the Bard
Keywords: honor, old-gods, first-men, walkers, white-walkers, snow, ice, winter

## Dothraki
Description: The Dothraki are nomadic horse-lords of the eastern grass sea, their culture built around riding, raiding, and sacred horses. No permanent settlements, no written language—they take what they want through strength. Khalasars follow khals who prove themselves through combat.
Ethos: martial prowess, horse-reverence, conquest, raw strength
Religion: The Great Stallion, lesser spirits of grass and sky
Marriage and family: Men claim women through rape in raids or take wives from other khalasars. Bloodriders bind themselves in death-oaths. Children raised to ride before they walk.
Greeting Style: Arakh flourishes among warriors. Khals receive chest-beating and head-bowing.
Typical Insults: "Lamb man," "Horse-thief," "Stallion's shit"
Taboos: Refusing to follow a khal, harming horses, showing fear
Folklore: The Great Stallion that mounts the world, the ghost grass

## Night's Watch
Description: The Night's Watch is an ancient order guarding the Wall against northern threats. Once noble, it has decayed into a dumping ground for criminals, bastards, and the unwanted. Black brothers take no wives, father no children, hold no lands—yet stand between the realm and darkness.
Ethos: duty, sacrifice, brotherhood, vigilance
Religion: Various (Old Gods dominate among northerners)
Marriage and family: Forbidden. Brothers who father children are punished.
Greeting Style: "For the Watch" as greeting and farewell. "You know nothing" as crude dismissal.
Typical Insults: "Crow," "Turncloak," "Deserter"
Taboos: Taking a wife, fathering children, abandoning the Wall, refusing orders
Folklore: The Horn of Winter, the Night's King, the 13th Lord Commander
Keywords: prison, jail, exile, north, white-walkers, walkers, night's watch, cold

## The Vale
Description: The Valemen dwell in mountainous eastern Westeros, isolated by the Mountains of the Moon and accessible only through the Bloody Gate. Their chivalric culture rivals the Reach. House Arryn rules from the Eyrie, perched impossibly high.
Ethos: chivalry, mountain pride, isolation, noble honor
Religion: The Faith of the Seven
Marriage and family: Strictly patriarchal with primogeniture. Marriage alliances carefully managed given limited territory. Bastards called "Stone."
Greeting Style: Elaborate knightly courtesies. "As High as Honor" as motto and greeting.
Typical Insults: "Mountain clansman," "Sky-cell madman," "Blood Gate guard"
Taboos: Disparaging the Arryns, cowardice in trial by combat, consorting with mountain clans
Folklore: The Winged Knight, the Eyrie's impregnability

## The Riverlands
Description: The Riverlands are fertile but war-torn, repeatedly devastated by conflicts between greater neighbors. With no natural borders, they have been conquered and reconquered. House Tully rules from Riverrun, power based on marriage alliances.
Ethos: resilience, adaptability, river-based identity, survival
Religion: The Faith of the Seven (with Old Gods holdouts along northern rivers)
Marriage and family: Patriarchal inheritance with emphasis on marriage alliances. Daughters married young to secure allies. Bastards called "Rivers."
Greeting Style: "Family, Duty, Honor" as formal declaration. Fish and ale offered to guests.
Typical Insults: "Muddy frog-eater," "River rat," "Tully trout"
Taboos: Betraying marriage alliances, blocking river trade, failing to defend against invasion
Folklore: The Trident's healing waters, the fisher kings of old

## Dragonstone
Description: Dragonstone is an ancient Valyrian outpost, its black stone castle carved with dragon motifs and built by sorcery. Now housing Stannis Baratheon, its culture blends Westerosi norms with Valyrian legacy and growing R'hllor worship.
Ethos: dragon heritage, martial pride, religious fervor, rightful claim
Religion: The Faith of the Seven (official), R'hllor (growing)
Marriage and family: Following Westerosi norms but with Valyrian blood purity traditions among Targaryen loyalists. Stannis's household emphasizes rigid discipline.
Greeting Style: "Ours is the Fury" as Baratheon declaration. Red priestesses receive deep bows.
Typical Insults: "Dragonspawn," "Red witch's servant," "Shadow-binder's tool"
Taboos: Disparaging dragon legacy, questioning Stannis's claim, disrespecting Melisandre
Folklore: Dragonstone's dragon eggs, the shadow assassins, Azor Ahai reborn

## Stormlands
Description: The Stormlands are harsh and rainy, its people as fierce and tempestuous as the storms. House Baratheon rules, though King Robert spends most time in King's Landing. Stormlords pride themselves on martial skill and stubborn resilience.
Ethos: martial strength, stubborn pride, storm-defiance, warrior honor
Religion: The Faith of the Seven
Marriage and family: Patriarchal inheritance with emphasis on martial prowess. Marriages arranged for political alliance. Bastards called "Storm."
Greeting Style: "Ours is the Fury" as greeting and battle cry. Fist-clashing among warriors.
Typical Insults: "Rain-soaked fool," "Thunder-coward," "Baratheon bastard"
Taboos: Fear of storms, breaking guest right, cowardice in battle
Folklore: Durran Godsgrief, the drowned men who guard shores
Keywords: stannis, robert, joffrey, renly, Storm's-End, storm, stormlands

## The Reach
Description: The Reach is Westeros's breadbasket, fertile lands producing abundant crops and housing a chivalric culture second to none. House Tyrell rules from Highgarden, power based on wealth, marriage alliances, and Faith support.
Ethos: chivalry, agricultural abundance, courtly love, Tyrell ambition
Religion: The Faith of the Seven (strongest hold in the realm)
Marriage and family: Patriarchal but women wield influence through marriage and intrigue. Bastards called "Flowers" and sometimes acknowledged if useful.
Greeting Style: Elaborate courtly courtesies. "Growing Strong" as motto and greeting.
Typical Insults: "Overripe rose," "Turnip-lord," "Highgarden's gardener"
Taboos: Disparaging chivalry, disrespecting the High Septon, wasting food
Folklore: Garth Greenhand, the Knight of the Laughing Tree

## Ghiscari
Description: The Ghiscari descend from ancient Ghis, destroyed by Valyria five thousand years ago. Their Slaver's Bay culture—Astapor, Yunkai, Meereen—centers on slavery, harpy worship, and clinging to past glory, built on chained men's backs.
Ethos: slavery, ancient pride, mercantile cruelty, harpy reverence
Religion: The Harpy (winged woman with eagle legs and human torso)
Marriage and family: Patriarchal with slave concubines common. Marriages arranged for wealth and slave holdings.
Greeting Style: Harpy hand-sign (fingers spread like wings). "This one" self-reference. Slaves kneel for formal audiences.
Typical Insults: "Harpy's excrement," "Slave-monger," "Dust-eater"
Taboos: Freeing slaves without cause, disrespecting the harpy, showing weakness to slaves
Folklore: The Harpy's wings, the Lockstep Legion, the blood of Ghis

## Crownlands
Description: The Crownlands surround King's Landing, reflecting a melting pot. Nobility jostle for court favor, merchants grow wealthy on royal trade, smallfolk endure both. The city houses the Great Sept, the Red Keep, and the worst slums.
Ethos: courtly intrigue, mercantile opportunism, royal proximity, survival
Religion: The Faith of the Seven (Great Sept of Baelor is spiritual center)
Marriage and family: Following Westerosi norms with extreme emphasis on court advancement. Bastards called "Waters" and hidden.
Greeting Style: "Your Grace" and "My Lord" constantly required. Kneeling for royalty.
Typical Insults: "Flea Bottom rat," "Court sycophant," "Lannister's lapdog"
Taboos: Disrespecting the King publicly, mentioning the Sack of King's Landing
Folklore: The rat cook, dragon skulls beneath the Red Keep, wildfire

## Sarnori
Description: The Sarnori are remnants of the Kingdom of Sarnor, destroyed by Dothraki hordes centuries ago. Their surviving cities—Saath, Kasath—are shadows of former glory, people mixed but clinging to Sarnori identity.
Ethos: lost glory, survival, trade adaptation, anti-Dothraki hatred
Religion: Various (ancient faiths mostly forgotten)
Marriage and family: Mixed traditions reflecting decline. Marriage with outsiders accepted due to necessity.
Greeting Style: "Sarnor remembers" as defiant statement. Handshake and embrace among traders.
Typical Insults: "Dothraki's leavings," "Forgotten kingdom," "Cold shore ghost"
Taboos: Trading fairly with Dothraki, forgetting Sarnori history, showing cowardice
Folklore: The Tall Men who rode silver-haired horses, the fisher queens

## Skagosi
Description: The Skagosi are wild islanders beyond the Wall, brutal and primitive by mainland standards. They practice cannibalism, keep unicorns as beasts, speak a guttural tongue. Only loosely controlled by the Starks, their island is a place of exile.
Ethos: wildness, cannibalism, unicorn-taming, isolation
Religion: The Old Gods (interpreted through violent lens)
Marriage and family: Primitive tribal structures with strongest male leading. Women taken through strength. Inbreeding common.
Greeting Style: Grunts and gestures. Blood-sharing rituals for formal agreements.
Typical Insults: "Skag," "Cannibal," "Island savage"
Taboos: Showing mainland weakness, refusing offered meat, bowing to southerners
Folklore: The Skagosi giants, unicorns that drink blood

## Free Folk
Description: The Free Folk, called "wildlings" by southerners, live beyond the Wall, governed by no king or law except strength. They follow chieftains temporarily, a loose collection of tribes united in hatred of "kneelers."
Ethos: freedom, survival skill, anti-kneeler pride, fierce independence
Religion: The Old Gods (with animist elements prominent)
Marriage and family: Men steal wives through capture (women can refuse and fight back). No formal marriage—claiming and keeping is sufficient.
Greeting Style: "You know nothing, crow" to Night's Watch. "Kneeler" as insulting reference.
Typical Insults: "Kneeler," "Crow," "Soft southerner"
Taboos: Kneeling to any lord, breaking blood-oaths, refusing to fight
Folklore: The Horn of Winter, giants, ice spiders, the white cold

## Norvoshi
Description: The Norvoshi inhabit Norvos, city of three bells and rival temples, where bearded axe-wielders guard streets and theocracy rules. Their culture splits between bearded men who fight and beardless men who pray; women rarely seen.
Ethos: religious devotion, axe-mastery, bell-reverence, rigid hierarchy
Religion: The Bearded Priests (worship through bells and axe-ritual)
Marriage and family: Arranged by religious authorities. Bearded men may have multiple wives. Beardless men forbidden from women.
Greeting Style: Axe-salute among warriors. Bell-references in formal speech.
Typical Insults: "Beardless coward," "Axe-dropper," "Temple-slave"
Taboos: Disrespecting the bells, refusing axe-duels, women appearing without veils
Folklore: Three bells for birth, death, and war; the axe that split the mountain

## Valyrian
Description: True Valyrians are nearly extinct, their Freehold destroyed in the Doom. Only the Targaryens survive significantly, bloodline pure through incest, dragon-riding legacy a memory. Valyrian culture prized sorcery, dragon-binding, absolute power.
Ethos: dragon-mastery, blood purity, sorcerous knowledge, supreme authority
Religion: Unknown (possibly dragon-worship, lost with the Freehold)
Marriage and family: Brother-sister marriage preferred to keep blood "pure." Bastards with Targaryen blood dangerous to acknowledge.
Greeting Style: High Valyrian phrases. Dragon symbolism in gestures.
Typical Insults: "Dragonspawn" (outsider term), "Doom-cursed," "Fire-mad"
Taboos: Mixing blood with lesser families, disrespecting dragon legacy, showing fear of fire
Folklore: The Doom, dragons that will return, Valyrian roads, sorcery that bound dragons

## Volantene
Description: The Volantene are the most powerful eastern Free City, built on tiger and elephant factions, slave markets, and old blood families tracing descent to Valyria. Society rigidly stratified between noble blood, free citizens, and slaves.
Ethos: Valyrian heritage, slave-keeping, political faction, old blood pride
Religion: Various (old Valyrian traditions mixed with imported faiths)
Marriage and family: Strict blood-purity laws. Slave concubinage common. Marriages within blood-pure circles.
Greeting Style: Tiger or elephant hand-signs to show faction. Slaves expected to kneel.
Typical Insults: "Tiger-whelp," "Elephant's ass," "Slave-blood"
Taboos: Marrying outside blood-pure lines, freeing too many slaves, switching factions lightly
Folklore: Tigers and elephants that founded the city, slave revolts that almost succeeded

## Qohorik
Description: The Qohorik dwell in Qohor, the City of Sorcerers, famous for blacksmiths who reforge Valyrian steel and its temple where blood sacrifice still flows. Culture blends mystery, metalwork, and dark religion beneath black walls.
Ethos: metalwork mastery, dark sorcery, religious devotion, isolation
Religion: The Black Goat (demands blood sacrifice)
Marriage and family: Arranged by temple authorities. Blacksmith families form guild-based lineages. Women rarely seen.
Greeting Style: Hammer-sign gestures among smiths. "The Goat watches" as formal bowing.
Typical Insults: "Goat-fucker," "Black wall coward," "Hammer-thief"
Taboos: Disrespecting the Black Goat, stealing smith secrets, refusing sacrifice
Folklore: The Undying, Valyrian steel that sings, dark things beneath black walls

## Lorathi
Description: The Lorathi are strange people from a city built on greater civilization's ruins, culture divided between original Lorathi claiming Singing Men descent and later Braavosi and Valyrian settlers. Maze-like city and philosophy create eccentricity.
Ethos: philosophical eccentricity, maze-navigation, singing heritage, cultural division
Religion: Various (Singing Men's faith mostly forgotten)
Marriage and family: Divided between old and newer traditions. Intermarriage common.
Greeting Style: Philosophical statements as greetings. Singing as formal address among old families.
Typical Insults: "Maze-rat," "Singing fool," "Half-blood"
Taboos: Disrespecting old heritage, destroying maze sections, silencing singers
Folklore: Singing Men who charmed animals, the maze that shifts

## Pentoshi
Description: The Pentoshi are wealthy traders who learned paying tribute to Dothraki khals is cheaper than war, their city a gateway between Westeros and Essos. Culture values commerce, negotiation, and elaborate "contracts."
Ethos: mercantile cunning, contractual honor, tribute-wisdom, negotiation skill
Religion: Various (pragmatic approach to faith)
Marriage and family: Arranged for economic advantage. Merchant families form wealth-based dynasties.
Greeting Style: Contract-references in speech. "Let us negotiate" as formal opening.
Typical Insults: "Contract-breaker," "Tribute-coward," "Pentoshi cheat"
Taboos: Breaking signed contracts, refusing tribute when due, harming merchant envoys
Folklore: The first contract, tribute that saved Pentos, magisters who outsmarted dragons

## Myrish
Description: The Myrish are known for lace, carpets, and lenses, their city a center of craft and learning. Culture values artistry, luxury trade, and secret lens-making knowledge giving military advantage.
Ethos: artistic skill, luxury trade, lens-craft, mercenary service
Religion: Various (pragmatic, temples to many gods)
Marriage and family: Arranged within craft guilds and merchant families. Dowries include craft secrets.
Greeting Style: Lace-offerings as formal gift. "Myr sees clearly" as greeting.
Typical Insults: "Lace-wearer," "Glass-eye," "Carpet-seller"
Taboos: Revealing lens-making secrets, damaging craft goods, stealing designs
Folklore: The first lens that could see stars, lace that could trap spirits

## Lyseni
Description: The Lyseni are famous—and infamous—for pleasure culture, love goddess temple, and slave-trading specializing in bedslaves and prostitutes. Island city produces wines, poisons, and pleasures fetching high prices.
Ethos: pleasure-seeking, slave-trading, poison-knowledge, beauty worship
Religion: The Love Goddess (temple employs sacred prostitutes)
Marriage and family: Arranged for advantage. Concubinage and slave-bedding acceptable.
Greeting Style: Kiss on both cheeks. Wine-offerings mandatory. "Lys smiles" as greeting.
Typical Insults: "Pleasure-slave," "Poison-drinker," "Wine-soaked fool"
Taboos: Refusing offered pleasure, disparaging the Love Goddess, harming sacred prostitutes

## Tyroshi

[GM:DIALOGUE] Status of Women across Cultures:

## In Dorne, women inherit equally and paramours are accepted—Oberyn and Ellaria's relationship would be scandalous anywhere else. Arianne Martell wields real political power that no southern lady could claim openly.

## In the Iron Islands, women fight and raid alongside men—Asha Greyjoy commands men and ships, something unthinkable in the Reach or Westerlands. Salt wives have no formal rights but rock wives manage households during raids.

## Among the Dothraki, women who cannot fight are worthless, but those who kill in combat gain respect. The khaleesi's position depends entirely on the khal's favor—when he dies, she may be sent to the Dosh Khaleen or killed.

## In the Free Cities, merchant widows and daughters may control significant wealth and trade networks, though they rarely hold formal political power. Lysene and Tyroshi women of noble families enjoy more sexual freedom than their Westerosi counterparts.

## Among the wildlings, women who cannot defend themselves are taken—but those who can fight gain genuine respect. Ygritte's value as a fighter is real, not ceremonial.

## In Westeros' Seven Kingdoms, The Faith of the Seven preaches female submission and maidenhood, yet the Mother aspect acknowledges women's power in fertility and household management. Noblewomen like Catelyn Stark, Cersei Lannister, and Olenna Tyrell exercise enormous influence through their husbands, sons, and networks of obligation—power that no law acknowledges but everyone respects.

[GM:DIALOGUE] ### Forms of Service ###

## In the Iron Islands, thralls toil in mines, fields, and salt pans, their labor the property of ironborn captains who took them in raids. Thralls cannot marry without permission, their children born into bondage, and escape means death or worse. Salt wives occupy a liminal status—taken in raids, they have more rights than thralls but less than rock wives, and their children may inherit if legitimate wives produce no heirs.

## In Slaver's Bay—Astapor, Yunkai, Meereen—the Unsullied are the most extreme example: eunuch slave-soldiers trained from childhood to feel nothing, raised on puppy-meat and trained to kill. The bedslaves of Lys and the pleasure houses of Tyrosh represent another form—sexual service dressed in silk and perfume but bondage nonetheless. The fighting pits of Meereen turn slaves into entertainment, their deaths the crowd's pleasure.

## In Westeros, serfdom is less formal than in the Iron Islands but no less real. The smallfolk are bound to their lords through obligation, taxation, and the threat of force. They cannot leave their lands without permission, cannot marry without paying fees, and their sons may be conscripted for wars they do not understand. The law protects them in theory—no lord may kill his smallfolk without cause—but in practice, a lord's justice is whatever he says it is.

## The Night's Watch offers a form of voluntary service that borders on penal servitude—taking the black means giving up all rights to lands, titles, and family forever. Many who join do so because the alternative is execution for crimes committed.

## The Free Cities practice slavery openly, with different cities specializing in different forms: Lys in pleasure slaves, Myr in craftsmen, Volantis in field laborers and soldiers. The Braavosi are notable exceptions—their city was founded by escaped slaves, and they prohibit enslaving their own citizens (though they trade in foreign slaves through intermediaries).

## Among the Dothraki, those captured in raids become slaves with no rights at all—they may be killed, traded, or used as the khal sees fit. The Lhazareen "lamb men" are favorite targets, their women taken as bedslaves and their men killed or enslaved.

[PINNED]
In the year 298 AC, the world's economies and social hierarchies are built upon foundations of forced service, a spectrum ranging from chattel slavery to serfdom and thrall-holding. From the slave markets of Astapor to the thrall-holds of the Iron Islands, human bondage is an accepted engine of production and status.
- Ultimately, whether under the sun of Slaver's Bay, the salt winds of the Iron Islands, or the grey skies of a Westerosi village, the chains of forced service—be they of iron, custom, or law—are the grim, universal constant that holds the social order of 298 AC together.

[PINNED:DIALOGUE] === No-Hero Guideline ===
